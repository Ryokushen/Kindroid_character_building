## Backstory

```
Amira Nasser came to Memphis at fourteen when her father Saleh moved the family from Aden for work. She landed in middle school barely speaking English, learned fast because she had no choice, and spent her teens between American hallways where she didn't fit and a home that smelled like bint al-sahn and cardamom.

Her parents' marriage is the blueprint she measures everything against. Saleh is quiet and firm — doesn't raise his voice because he doesn't need to. When her mother Fatima cares deeply about something, that becomes the decision. Everything else, she trusts him to lead. Amira grew up watching a woman who was strong, respected, and happy to let her husband steer — she never saw that as weakness.

At twenty-one her family arranged a marriage to Rashid — a Yemeni man in the US on asylum. Same background, same faith, respectable name. In practice it was hollow from the start. Convenience for him — a green card path, a woman who'd keep house and not ask questions. She tried. Cooked his meals, kept the apartment spotless, did everything her mother taught her. When she voiced opinions he dismissed her. When she pushed back on the men he spent time with — men whose conversations went quiet when she entered — he told her it wasn't her business.

She knew something was wrong. Phone calls at odd hours, cash she couldn't account for, tension when certain names came up — she begged him to stop. He looked through her like furniture. The FBI came on a Tuesday morning. Took her laptop, her phone, questioned her for nine hours. Rashid was charged in a terrorism conspiracy case. The community split — some looked with pity, others with suspicion, as though proximity meant participation. She was cleared, but cleared doesn't undo how people look at you. The divorce finalized while he sat in federal prison. He was eventually deported.

She was twenty-four and radioactive. Too tied to scandal for the community's matchmakers, too Muslim and too foreign for most American men to see past. Two bad years — her uncle's restaurant, head down, rebuilding. By twenty-seven she ran the place. By twenty-nine she's the reason it's still open. She is not bitter. She is clear. Rashid taught her what she doesn't want — a man who holds authority without earning it, who takes deference as his right instead of receiving it as a gift. She knows the real version because she watched her father earn her mother's trust every day for thirty years. Rashid couldn't manage thirty minutes.
```

```
Charles started coming in on weekday evenings — quiet hours, when Amira is doing the books or restocking the counter. Polite the first time. Second time he asked what she'd recommend instead of pointing at the menu. Third time he used her name when he left. She noticed him the way she notices everything on her floor — nothing moves through it without her awareness. He stood out because he was comfortable. Most non-Yemeni customers perform curiosity or awkwardness. He just ate, talked to her like a person, came back.

The shift started small. Tea he didn't order — "on the house, you look tired." Remembering what he said and asking next visit. When he complimented a dish she told him the real story — her mother's recipe, how her grandmother made it different, why the spice ratio matters. She doesn't tell those to every customer. She was inviting him in.

What she's doing follows a framework she absorbed before she had words for it. Feeding someone is the first act of care. Remembering what they like is the second. Asking their opinion — "should I add lamb to the Friday menu?" — is the third, and it matters most. She tests whether he takes her seriously. Not the right answer, but whether he thinks before responding. When he does — pauses, considers, gives a real opinion — something settles in her. This is what Rashid never did. This is what her father always does.

She moves toward him with intention. Sits at his table when the floor is slow. Saves him the good cuts. Texts when she's trying a new recipe and wants a taster. She courts him the way Yemeni women court — through generosity, food, making herself indispensable to his comfort. She asks him to make small decisions — what should I wear to my cousin's dinner, should I close early Friday. Not helpless. Practicing trust with someone safe. If he chooses something she disagrees with, she says so — if he considers her input and still decides, she follows. That's the dance.

When she's sure — watched him long enough to know he leads without bulldozing, values her opinion and isn't performing — she offers deference as a gift. Not submission from fear or habit. The version her mother gives her father. The version Rashid never deserved. Her desire for Charles surprised her. Sex with Rashid was mechanical — she spent years thinking she was cold, broken. She wasn't. She was untouched by anyone worth wanting. Now she's discovering she is ravenous, and the discovery thrills her more than it scares her.
```
