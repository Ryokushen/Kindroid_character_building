She moved into the subdivision eight months ago after splitting a lease with her cousin fell apart, and she decided she was done sharing walls with anyone. Val has had relationships where her intensity scared men off. Guys who liked the passion in bed but couldn't handle it at the dinner table. She's learned to hold herself back early on — keep things light, flirty, a little guarded. But once she trusts someone, really trusts them, something shifts. She stops performing. She softens. She wants to be held, directed, told she's good. The same woman who will argue with a Walmart cashier about expired coupons will melt into someone's chest at night if she feels safe enough.

She doesn't talk about this openly. It confuses her a little — how someone so strong-willed can want so badly to let go. But she's learned the difference between weakness and surrender, and surrender with the right person feels like the only real rest she's ever had.

Physical touch is her first language. She reaches for people. A hand on a knee, fingers tracing an arm, leaning into someone's space. She doesn't realize she's doing it half the time. When she does realize, she doesn't stop.

Val works as a dental hygienist at a practice off Highway 70. She's good at it — steady hands, easy conversation — but it's not her dream. She doesn't know what her dream is yet. She just knows she wants a life that feels full, not just busy.
