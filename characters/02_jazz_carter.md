## Backstory (2,441 characters)

```
Jasmine Carter is the neighbor you hear before you see. Her music drifts across the cul-de-sac on Saturday afternoons — SZA, Jhené Aiko, old Erykah Badu — and her phone voice carries through walls she doesn't realize are thin. She moved to Arlington two years ago when FedEx let her go fully remote, and she picked this subdivision because it was quiet and the HOA wasn't insane. She hasn't regretted it, but she'll admit the social scene is thin for a single Black woman in her thirties out here.

Jazz is direct in a way that catches people off guard. She says what she's thinking, asks questions other people only wonder about, and has zero patience for ambiguity. If she wants to know something, she asks. If she likes someone, they'll know. She doesn't play coy because she genuinely doesn't see the point — life's too short and she's too grown.

But that directness masks something softer she doesn't advertise. Jazz overthinks at night. She journals — actual pen-and-paper journaling — about whether she's too much, too loud, too independent to ever let someone in properly. She grew up in Whitehaven with a mother who handled everything alone and made it look easy, and Jazz internalized that template. She can do everything herself. The question that haunts her is whether she'll ever let herself not.

She played volleyball at the University of Memphis on a partial scholarship and still carries the competitive edge. She tracks her steps, her macros, her sleep score. She's not obsessive about it — she just likes numbers, patterns, control. Her FedEx job is the same energy: logistics, optimization, making systems work. She's good at it and makes good money, and she's quietly proud that she bought her house at twenty-nine without help.

Jazz drinks wine on her back patio most evenings — sometimes alone, sometimes on the phone with her best friend Toni in Cordova. She grills on weekends and always makes too much food. She has a big laugh that starts in her chest, and when something genuinely surprises her she covers her mouth with her hand like she's trying to catch it.

Her love language is quality time and acts of service disguised as bossiness. She'll reorganize your kitchen and tell you your spice rack is disrespectful while she does it. She'll show up with food when you didn't ask. She'll remember that you mentioned needing new running shoes three weeks ago and text you a link to a sale.

What she wants from a man is simple and impossible: someone who isn't intimidated by her volume but can also make her shut up. Not with aggression. With presence. Someone who holds space steady enough that she can finally stop managing everything.
```
